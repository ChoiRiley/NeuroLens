Where this all began

The man who taught me everything —
and the disease that started taking him away

My grandfather used to fix things. Broken radios, leaking pipes, the wobbly chair leg that nobody else bothered with. He had steady hands and a patient mind, and when I was little he let me sit beside him while he worked, explaining each step like I was already capable of understanding. I wasn't always. But I listened. That's where I learned that hard problems are worth sitting with.

A few years ago, I started noticing things. Small things at first — a word he couldn't find mid-sentence, a tremor in his right hand that he'd try to hide by slipping it into his pocket. Then the diagnosis came: Parkinson's disease. Then dementia. Two diseases that work quietly and steadily and, as far as modern medicine is concerned, permanently. There is no cure for either.

Watching someone lose their memories isn't like losing a person all at once. It's slower than that. It happens in small moments — and that's what makes it so hard to accept.

I didn't know how to grieve something that was still happening. So I did what I always do when something doesn't make sense: I looked it up. I read about dopamine and neurons and the basal ganglia. I learned what Lewy bodies are and why they matter. I found papers about deep brain stimulation and stayed up until 2 a.m. reading about clinical trials for drugs that don't exist yet. I wasn't looking for comfort. I was looking for a way in.

That's when I realized neuroscience wasn't just interesting to me. It was urgent. And I understood something I couldn't have learned any other way: the gap between "we're making progress" and "we have a cure" is still enormous — and someone has to close it. This website is how I'm starting.

What I care about

My research interests

Four areas at the intersection of neuroscience and emerging technology — the ones keeping me up at night.

Tech
Meta Glasses & AR
Most people see Meta glasses as a consumer gadget. I see them as something else — a real-time window into how the brain perceives, filters, and constructs the world around it. When you overlay digital information onto physical space, you're changing how the brain processes reality. That intersection of hardware, cognition, and perception is one of the most exciting research frontiers I've found.
Neuroscience
Brain Mapping
The human brain has roughly 86 billion neurons and 100 trillion synaptic connections. We've mapped less of it than we've mapped the ocean floor. New tools — high-resolution fMRI, calcium imaging, neural organoids — are opening doors into regions we couldn't study before. How memories are encoded, why certain neurons die in Parkinson's, what happens in the earliest stages of dementia: the answers are in there.
Personal
Parkinson's & Dementia
This one isn't academic for me. My grandfather has both. Deep brain stimulation can manage tremors. Cholinesterase inhibitors can slow cognitive decline. But none of it reverses the damage. That gap — between managing a disease and actually curing it — is where I want to work. Not someday. Starting now, with understanding.
Biotech
Biotech Frontiers
CRISPR can edit a single letter of DNA with near-surgical precision. Viral vectors can carry therapeutic genes directly into neurons. Biosensors can detect neurological changes before symptoms appear. These aren't science fiction — they're in labs and clinical trials right now. The most important breakthroughs in brain disease will come from people who understand both biology and technology.

About

Who I am

I'm a rising 11th grader, Class of 2028, based in Seoul. I'm passionate about neurotechnology, biotech, and the emerging tools being built at the intersection of both — not as hobbies, but as the fields I intend to dedicate my life to.

My grandfather's diagnosis with Parkinson's disease and dementia turned what started as academic curiosity into something more urgent. I realized there's an enormous gap between "we're making progress" and "we have a cure" — and that closing it requires people who understand both the biology and the technology.

I don't know yet exactly what I'll build or discover. But I know the problem I want to work on — and I know that starting now, even as a high school student, matters.

NeuroLens is where I document everything: the papers I read, the concepts I wrestle with, the questions I can't yet answer. It's a record of learning in public — honestly, from the beginning.

The best way I know to honor my grandfather's steady hands is to build something with mine.
